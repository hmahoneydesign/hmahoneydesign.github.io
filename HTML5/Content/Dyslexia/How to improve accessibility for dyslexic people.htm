How to improve accessibility for dyslexic people

More utilization of audiobooks for students

Many schools often underutilize the potential of audiobooks, which can significantly enhance learning experiences, particularly for dyslexic students. Audiobooks not only improve comprehension but also save valuable time by allowing students to engage with the material more effectively. By investing more resources and effort into converting textbooks into audiobooks, we could likely see an increase in grades and overall academic performance. Currently, many schools have access to the Libby app, which allows students to borrow audiobooks for free through their library systems. To maximize the benefits of this resource, it would be essential to promote Libby more actively among teachers and to expand the selection of available titles. By increasing awareness and accessibility of audiobooks within the classroom, we can create a more inclusive learning environment for all students. Additionally, in cases where students need to read online content instead of traditional textbooks, it is crucial to provide readily accessible speech-to-text software. Installing these tools on school computers and offering training sessions to students and educators would greatly enhance comprehension for those with dyslexia, leading to improved learning outcomes. Furthermore, integrating audiobooks and assistive technologies into the curriculum could foster a more engaging and dynamic classroom experience. Teachers can implement strategies that encourage students to listen to audiobooks in tandem with reading physical texts, promoting better retention of information and deeper understanding of the material.

Improving voice recognition in voice to text

Although many of these tools are becoming more commonplace in writing applications and general communication software, their accessibility is often not as great as one might expect. A prime example of this is voice-to-text technology, which relies on vocal recognition software. This software must be trained on various voices so that the algorithm can accurately transcribe spoken words into text. However, the initial training for many voice-to-text programs predominantly used adult male voices. As a result, these programs struggle to recognize voices with different registers and intonations, leaving many young people and individuals with feminine voices at a disadvantage. This inaccuracy can require dyslexic individuals and others to repeatedly review their transcriptions to ensure correctness, which undermines the very purpose of these accessibility tools.

Another important aspect of accessibility is found in screen readers. While many screen readers provide plain and simple readings, they often lack the intonation that conveys meaning and emotion in text. By improving the use of alt text, including features like bold, underline, and italics, we can enhance intonation cues for screen readers. This improvement would significantly benefit comprehension, especially for dyslexic readers.

Although these solutions may seem tailored specifically for dyslexic individuals, they would also assist a wide range of people, including those who are neurodivergent. For instance, individuals with ADHD or autism often struggle to maintain focus on a single task for extended periods. This is especially true when reading material that may not align with their interests. By providing access to audiobooks and more dynamic screen readers for neurodivergent individuals, we allow them to listen and absorb information while engaging in other activities that aid their comprehension, such as using fidget tools or doodling.
